Breakbeat

Breakbeat is a music production method that uses a sampler or editing software to disassemble
the phrases of a drum performance and reassemble them in a sequencer, as well as a music genre that accompanies that method.

The tempo of breaks tracks, ranging from 110 to 150 beats per minute, allows DJs to mix breaks with a wide range of different genres in their sets.
This has led to breakbeats being used in many hip hop, jungle/drum & bass and hardcore tracks. They can also be heard
in other music, anywhere from popular music to background music in car and clothing commercials on radio or TV.

UK Garage

UK garage, abbreviated as UKG, is a genre of electronic dance music which originated in England in the early to mid-1990s.
The genre was most clearly inspired by garage house, but also incorporates elements from dance-pop, R&B, and jungle.
It is defined by percussive, shuffled rhythms with syncopated hi-hats, cymbals, and snares, and may include either 4/4 house kick patterns
or more irregular "2-step" rhythms. Garage tracks also commonly feature 'chopped up' and time-stretched or
pitch-shifted vocal samples complementing the underlying rhythmic structure at a tempo usually around 130 BPM.

House Music

House music is the collective term for several styles of electronic dance music
that originated in the mid-1980s.
The etymology of house is said to have been derived from a warehouse club
where Frankie Knuckles, a DJ in Chicago who mixed old disco music with
Euro synthpop music, was active.

House is a music genre characterized by a repetitive four-on-the-floor beat
and a typical tempo of 120 beats per minute.
It was created by DJs and music producers from Chicago's underground club culture
in the early/mid 1980s,
as DJs began altering disco songs to give them a more mechanical beat.

House Music Keyword : 4/4 beat, basic 120bpm, Chicago Ware House, Frankie Knuckles, Chicago House

Deep House

Deep house is a subgenre of electronic house music that originated in the 1980s,
and was initially a fusion genre resulting from the contact between Chicago house and
jazz funk or soul music in the 1980s.
Its origins are traced to Larry Heard's 1984 track "The Mystery of Love".

Deep house is typically characterized by a BPM of 110 to 125.
Quiet basslines,extensive use of percussion (typically a Roland TR-909 drum machine),
smooth keyboard sounds, advanced chord structures, ambient mixes, and soulful female vocals.
The lyrics use positive or lonely modern blues lyrics. Deep house continues
the use of vocals as a new form of house music often abandoned.
However, in 2019, these differences have largely disappeared.
